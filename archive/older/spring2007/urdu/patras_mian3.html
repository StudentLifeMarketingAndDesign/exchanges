Three or four days ago in the morning Roshanara asked me if she could go to her mother’s house as she has visited her mother’s only twice since our wedding. She asked this with such unassumingness that it was impossible to refuse her. “So I’ll go on the one thirty train?” she asked. “As you like,” I answered.
She quickly set about getting ready, and I began to fantasize about my upcoming freedom: I’d invite my friends over and make a lot of noise; I’d eat whatever I want, get up whenever I want, and make sure to go see a play.
“Roshanara, hurry up or you’ll miss your train,” I called out.
I took her to the station and she found her seat. “Write often,” she said.
“Every day. And you too.”
“Eat regular meals. Clean socks and hankies are in the bottom drawer of the dresser.”
Then we fell silent and began staring at each other. Tears filled her eyes, and this had its effect on me too. When the train set off, I stood on the platform momentarily stupefied.
Finally I sidled away and ended up at a bookstore. I flipped through magazines, looking at the pictures. I bought a newspaper. I folded this into my pocket and mechanically turned toward home.
But then I realized I didn’t have to go home. I could go anywhere I wanted. If I wanted to, I could go back to the station to wander around for hours. Or if I wanted, I could do somersaults.
It’s said that if savages are brought to a developed country, they’ll be very impressed with the marvelous sights they see. But when they return to their jungles, they’ll yell out for joy. I was feeling something akin to this. I ran from the station without a care in the world. I blithely called out to a tanga driver, and when he pulled his horses up, I jumped on and we took off down the street. I lit a cigarette. I stretched my legs out over the seat and pointed him in the direction of the club.
On the way I remembered something very important. I told the driver to turn toward my house. Once there, I called out to my servant.
“Amjad?”
“Yes, sir.”
“Would you go over to the barber and tell him to come by tomorrow at eleven.”
“Yes.”
“Eleven o’clock — you heard me, right? Not like how he’ll show up at six, okay?
“Yes, sir.”
“And if he comes before eleven, hit him over the head and toss him into the street.”
After that I went to the club. Up till then I’d never gone to the club at two in the afternoon. I went inside to find it completely desolate. There was no sign of anyone. I looked in every room: the billiards room was empty; the chess room was empty; the card room was empty. The only person I found was a servant sharpening knives in the dining room.
“Hey, you — no one came today?”
“Sir, as you know, no one comes at this time.”
I was very disappointed. I went outside to think about what I should do next. When I couldn’t think of anything else, I set out for Mirza’s house. I learned he wasn’t back yet from his office, and so I went there. When he saw me, he was very surprised. I told him what was going on.
